# Optimization: Gradients & Learning Rates

This document details how we navigate the error space of models, framing optimization concepts by the **problems they solve**.

---

## 1. Navigating the Error Curve

### **Problem: How do we know which direction and how far to adjust weights to reduce loss?**
* **Solution: Derivatives and Slope**
  * **Why it exists:** Adjusting weights randomly (guessing) is too slow. The derivative tells us exactly how the Loss ($L$) changes if we make a tiny adjustment to a weight ($w$).
  * **Visual Intuition**: 
    Imagine standing on a foggy hill. You want to reach the bottom (minimum loss). You cannot see the bottom, but you can feel the slope of the ground beneath your feet. Stepping in the direction *opposite* to the slope will lead you downhill.

```
       Loss (L)
        \
         \   <-- Current Weight State (High Loss)
          \
           \____
                \____ <-- Minimum Loss (Goal)
```

## 2. Gradient Descent Concepts

### **Problem: How do we measure the slope of multiple weights simultaneously?**
* **Solution: Gradient ($\nabla$)**
  * **Why it exists:** Models rarely have only one weight. The gradient is a vector (collection) of partial derivatives for every weight in the model.
  * **Vector Representation**:
    $$\nabla L = \left[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1}, \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_2}, \dots, \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_n} \right]$$
  * **Interpretation:** The gradient vector points in the direction of the *steepest ascent* (fastest increase in loss). Therefore, we subtract the gradient to step in the direction of *steepest descent*.

### **Problem: How do we control the size of our adjustments so we don't overshoot or get stuck?**
* **Solution: Learning Rate ($\alpha$)**
  * **Why it exists:** The gradient only tells us the direction of change. The learning rate scales the step size.
  * **Update Rule**:
    $$w \leftarrow w - \alpha \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial w}$$
  * **The Trade-off**:
    * **Too Small $\alpha$**: Tiny steps. Convergence takes extremely long and can get stuck in minor local minima.
    * **Too Large $\alpha$**: Overshoots the minimum. The loss will increase, causing the optimization to explode (divergence).

```
Too Large Learning Rate (Overshooting):
        \      /
         \    /
          \  /
           \/
   Step 1: Left side
   Step 2: Overshoots to the far right (higher up!)
   Step 3: Explodes outward
```
